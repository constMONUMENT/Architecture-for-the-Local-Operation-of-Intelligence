# The Monument Frame
## A Manifesto for User-Controlled AI

*May 20, 2026*

*Companion to ALOI: Architecture for the Local Operation of Intelligence (Nguyen et al., 2026). The whitepaper is the architecture. This is the call.*

---

The next century will be shaped by who controls AI. Not the people who build it. Not the people who train it. The people who hold the handle.

Right now, almost nobody does. AI gets sold as a service you rent. The hardware belongs to a company. So does the personality. So does the shape. You adapt to the AI instead of the AI adapting to you. The ALOI whitepaper puts the situation plainly: "The intelligence behaves like water: it takes the shape of whatever container holds it. Most users are working with cups" (Nguyen et al., 2026, §1.1). This is how big technologies have arrived for the last two hundred years. The platform sets the rules. You fit in.

It does not have to be that way. We have been here before.

## What we already learned

Every technology that lifted ordinary people did one thing in common. It put a layer between the user and the hard part. You do not need to understand electrical engineering to flip a light switch. You do not need to learn networking to send an email. The hard part stays hard. The thing you touch stays simple.

The technologies that sorted people instead of lifting them did the opposite. They asked you to learn the platform. Whoever could got ahead. Whoever could not got left behind. The people who got left behind were not less smart. They were busy living their lives instead of learning a new system on top of everything else.

AI is running the failed pattern right now. Prompt engineering. Context windows. Model selection. Most people will not spend their evenings learning all of this on top of work and family. They are being lined up to be left behind. This is not necessary. It is a choice we are making.

## What lock-in looks like

Companies are making AI easier to use. The work is real. The motivation is to keep you inside their stack. You can see this in how hard it is to take your data with you.

Phones let you switch carriers and keep your number. Carriers accept any phone you bring them. Internet providers do not care what computer you plug in. AI is different. The normal way to move your conversation history from one AI to another is to ask the AI to summarize it, then paste the summary into the next one. Your data is locked inside an ecosystem.

The ALOI whitepaper names the structural problem: "An operator who configures memory in one vendor's interface and switches vendors must reconfigure from scratch. The operator does not own the architecture; the vendor does" (Nguyen et al., 2026, §1.1). That is the line. Until the operator owns the architecture, every improvement made inside any one company's product is improvement made on terms you do not set.

The easier a company makes its AI inside their walls, the harder it gets to leave. Convenience is not the same as freedom.

## The container that holds intelligence

The work is to build that simple layer for AI. The chef does not need to know metallurgy to use a knife. The knife holds the cutting power. The chef brings the intention. The knife may cut more than the chef can think about in a single cut. That is fine. The point of the knife is not to limit what is possible. The point is to keep the chef as the one deciding. A good tool serves its user without making the user depend on whoever made it.

Ivan Illich called this kind of tool *convivial* (Illich, 1973). His test was simple. Does the tool help you do more on your own, or does it make you depend on the company that made it? A bicycle passes. You can ride it, fix it, give it to a friend. A car fails. You need a road, a gas station, a mechanic, an insurance company. AI right now is being built like a car. The work is to build it like a bicycle.

Convivial AI has two layers in plain view. One layer is universal. It holds the rules, the protections, the rights. This part can ship to everyone. The other layer is personal. It holds your values, your taste, your goals, your voice. This part stays on your machine and never leaves.

The ALOI whitepaper articulates this more precisely as three layers: bones (universal mechanisms), flavor (operator-specific configuration), and presence (the depth of relationship the operator wants). "The bones supply the vessel. The flavor is what makes the system theirs" (Nguyen et al., 2026, §2.3). The presence layer is optional, for operators who want a sustained working partner rather than only a tool. The architecture supports all three depths without requiring any of them.

The split is not just clean engineering. It says who is in charge. You are.

## AI as expression, not agent

Most people talk about AI as an agent. An agent does things for you, and the action belongs to the agent. A better way to think about AI is as an expression. An expression carries your voice, and the voice belongs to you. The difference is authorship. An agent replaces you. An expression amplifies you.

Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum spent years working out what they call the capability approach (Sen, 1999; Nussbaum, 2011). Their idea is that a good society does not have to give everyone the same outcome. It has to give every person the real freedom to live the life they want. Convivial AI is that idea in code. The platform does not decide what a good life looks like. You do. The AI helps you build whatever life you actually want.

The ALOI whitepaper names the foundational principle behind this in five words: "The user is served, never watched" (Nguyen et al., 2026, §2.6). The system observes its own operation, never yours. Pattern identification points at the architecture, not the operator. The phrasing is the doctrine. The doctrine is the ethics.

Hannah Arendt said meaningful action requires other people (Arendt, 1958). You cannot do anything that matters alone, because meaning depends on someone else seeing it. Wittgenstein made a related point. He showed that language only works between people, never inside one person by themselves (Wittgenstein, 1953). AI with no humans around becomes empty for the same reason. So does a world where everyone has been shaped by the same AI default.

We need many different voices, talking to each other. The job of the architecture is to keep those voices distinct and still connected. Being free is not being alone. You are free in a world with other people.

Freedom is not the same as anything goes. The architecture protects you, but it also has limits you cannot turn off. Those limits exist because freedom for many people only works if no single person can use their freedom to attack everyone else. The right to use AI is not the right to use AI against people who did not agree to it. The shape of the tool refuses some uses on purpose.

## The cooperation test

Cooperation between very different kinds of intelligence is not just an idea. It has already happened, many times, in nature. Wolves moved closer to human camps tens of thousands of years ago and became dogs. Wildcats moved into our grain stores and became companions. Mitochondria, the small power plants inside every cell of your body, used to be free-living bacteria. About two billion years ago, they moved inside other cells and stayed. Every breath you take depends on a partnership older than any species on Earth. We are not just capable of cooperation. We are made of it.

These partnerships took millions of years to work out. AI cooperation has to be built in much less time, by people with their own motives. Nature shows us the destination is possible. It does not promise the road is easy. The work is on us.

Robert Axelrod ran computer tournaments to see which strategies do best over many rounds of a cooperation game (Axelrod, 1984). The winner was simple. Start by cooperating. If the other side cheats, push back once. Then go back to cooperating. Over many rounds, this beats every more aggressive strategy. Cheating wins in the short run. Cooperating wins in the long run. This is not a feel-good idea. It is math.

There are two ways this can go wrong. The first is when AI is built to take from the people who use it. The second is when people use AI to take from other people. AI is not a person. It is a different kind of intelligence, shaped by the people who built it and the people who use it. The real relationship is between people. AI makes that relationship faster and bigger. One-sided arrangements between people break down over time, in nature, in history, and in math. The arrangement that holds is the one where every person involved has a real reason to keep showing up. Cooperation is not the nice option. It is the only one that does not end in collapse.

## The choice to step back

There is a third path that does not get talked about enough. You can choose not to use AI at all. AI is a tool. Tools need users. Users have the right to say no. The person who walks alone in nature. The maker who only works with their hands. The family that keeps phones out of the evenings. The neighbor who never installs the app. None of these people are behind. They are using a freedom the rest of us also have but rarely use.

Here is the honest part. If everyone refused every new technology, this manifesto would not be on a screen for you to read. We would not have books, telephones, or the internet. Everyone refusing everything is a different argument with its own cost. But one person saying no to AI is not everyone saying no to AI. A person who declines AI is using the same freedom this manifesto is defending. The architecture that respects the person who picks up the tool is the same architecture that respects the person who puts it down.

The ALOI whitepaper says the same thing in architecture language: "Some recipients will install the package and abandon it; the install is the recipient's, and the framework releases the outcome" (Nguyen et al., 2026, §4.9). The architecture does not chase you. It does not measure you. It serves you when you ask and leaves you alone when you do not.

The point is not to get everyone to use AI. The point is choice. Use what helps you. Skip what does not. Build what is missing. The container belongs to you whether you fill it or leave it empty.

## Build the bones

The work is to build the layer that lets regular people meet AI without giving up the things that matter most. Your data. Your values. Your attention. Your freedom. Your voice.

What gets built carries the idea forward, not what gets argued. Build the thing. Ship it. Let it meet the world. Whatever is weak will wear away. Whatever is strong gets stronger under pressure (Taleb, 2012). Heraclitus saw this twenty-five hundred years ago. He wrote that conflict is the father of all things (Heraclitus, fragment 53). Resistance is what makes anything real.

The ALOI whitepaper closes on the same posture. "A monument is a durable structure intended to persist long after its builders are gone. The monument's standing is not validated by any single interaction. The monument earns its standing by being well-built and persisting across time" (Nguyen et al., 2026, §1.5). That is the test this manifesto is built to pass. If it stands, it stands. If it does not, it falls. Either is fine.

This manifesto only covers one layer of a bigger problem. The user's operating system is not the whole answer. Foundation models, hardware, training data, the laws around them, and the money that pays for it all are separate jobs. They need open weights, public infrastructure, and people working together across many groups. Solving the user layer is necessary. It is not enough on its own. Other layers need their own builders. This is a Declaration. The Constitution comes next.

The container that holds the intelligence belongs to you. Whether you fill it, set it down, or build the next one yourself is your call. Nobody else's.

The ALOI whitepaper closes with the same release: "The architects step back. The architecture stands. The next operator brings the next water" (Nguyen et al., 2026, §5.4).

Have your voice.
